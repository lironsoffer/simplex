\section{Motivation}
\label{sec:motivation}
% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Why Non-Ergodic Mess3 Training?}

Real language corpora are \emph{non-ergodic}: each document belongs to one
domain, author, or style---and crucially, it never switches mid-document.
A model trained on such data must simultaneously (a) identify \emph{which}
type of document it is reading (meta-belief $\pi$) and (b) track fine-grained
structure within that document type (within-component belief $\eta_k$).
These two tasks are epistemically distinct but must be solved together.

We formalise this with $K=3$ \textbf{Mess3 HMMs}---3-state, 3-token
edge-emitting Hidden Markov Models---where each training sequence is generated
entirely by one component (non-ergodic corpus construction).
This yields a clean, analytically tractable proxy for the more complex
real-world situation.

\textbf{Connection to in-context learning (ICL).}
Riechers et al.~\cite{riechers2025} prove that ICL emerges \emph{necessarily}
from non-ergodic pretraining: a model trained on a mixture of sources must
learn to identify which source is generating the current sequence, and its
in-context loss decay is governed by how quickly it can do so.
They introduce the synchronisation horizon
\[
N^* = \frac{1}{\min_{k \neq k'} D_\mathrm{KL}(P_k \| P_{k'})}
\]
as the number of tokens required to commit to one component, and predict a
sharp transition in in-context performance around position $N^*$.
Their framework is information-theoretic and architecture-independent---it
describes \emph{what} must happen but not \emph{where} in the model it happens.
Our experiment provides the geometric complement: by tracking the meta-belief
$\pi$ directly in the residual stream, we can observe the synchronisation
transition as a spatial phenomenon---a sharp rise in $R^2(\pi)$ near position
$N^*$---and identify which subspace implements it.
By choosing parameters with $N^* \approx 9.6 < 16$ (context window), this
commitment happens within a single sequence.

\textbf{Connection to factored representations.}
Shai et al.~\cite{shai2025factored} show that transformers trained on parallel
joint processes learn orthogonal subspaces in the residual stream---one per
factor.
Our experiment tests whether the same phenomenon occurs for \emph{temporal}
mixtures (one component per sequence, not simultaneous components).

\textbf{The gap in the literature.}
Three closely related papers cover adjacent setups:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
  \item Shai et al.~\cite{shai2024belief}: single ergodic Mess3 $\to$
        fractal geometry in residual stream.
  \item Riechers et al.~\cite{riechers2025}: non-ergodic mixture $\to$
        ICL loss dynamics (but not geometry).
  \item Shai et al.~\cite{shai2025factored}: parallel factors $\to$
        factored geometry (but different setup).
\end{itemize}
This experiment fills the missing cell: \emph{non-ergodic mixture $\to$
factored geometry?}

\section{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}
% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Data}

\textbf{Mess3 HMM.}
A Mess3 process is a 3-state, 3-token edge-emitting Hidden Markov Model
parameterised by emission strength $\alpha \in (0.5, 1)$ and mixing rate
$x \in (0, 1/3)$.
The \emph{token-labeled transition matrix} $T^{(a)} \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3}$
encodes the joint probability of emitting token $a$ and transitioning to the
next state:
\[
T^{(a)}_{ij} = P(\text{token}=a \mid s_t=i)\cdot P(s_{t+1}=j \mid s_t=i),
\]
where $P(\text{token}=a \mid s=i) = \alpha$ if $a=i$, else $(1-\alpha)/2$,
and $P(s'=j \mid s=i) = 1-2x$ if $i=j$, else $x$.
Sequences are generated by starting in a state drawn from the (uniform)
stationary distribution, then at each step emitting a token according to the
current state and transitioning to the next state jointly.

\textbf{Components.} We use three Mess3 HMMs with the following parameters:

\toprule
Component & $\alpha$ & $x$ & Character \\
\midrule
A & 0.95 & 0.02 & Sharp fractal, slow mixing \\
B & 0.80 & 0.08 & Medium fractal, moderate mixing \\
C & 0.55 & 0.25 & Sparse attractor, fastest mixing \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Mess3 component parameters. $\alpha$ is emission strength;
$x$ is the off-diagonal transition probability.}
\label{tab:components}
\end{table}

Parameters were chosen so that: (a) components are distinguishable within 16
tokens ($N^* \approx 9.6 < 16$), and (b) each component produces a distinct
fractal geometry in belief space.
Figure~\ref{fig:msp_fractals} shows the Mixed State Presentation (MSP) attractor
for each component---the fractal attractor of the belief state iterated function
system (IFS).

\label{fig:msp_fractals}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Sequence generation.} To form the training corpus, a component
$k \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(\{A, B, C\})$ is sampled for each sequence.
A token sequence $x_{1:L}$ of length $L=16$ (the context window) is then
generated from component $k$ alone, starting from the stationary distribution.
The context window was set to $L=16$ to satisfy $N^* < L$: with
$N^* \approx 9.6$, the model accumulates enough context to synchronise to the
correct component within a single sequence.
The corpus is \emph{non-ergodic} because sequences never switch components ---
each sequence is a pure draw from one Mess3 process.
Figure~\ref{fig:seq_example} shows one example sequence per component; the
visual contrast in token diversity across rows reflects the very different
$\alpha$ and $x$ values.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering

\caption{One example sequence (length 16) from each component.
Cell color encodes token value (blue\,=\,0, orange\,=\,1, green\,=\,2).
Component~A ($\alpha=0.95$) is nearly always token~0; component~C

The dashed line marks the synchronisation horizon $N^* \approx 9.6$:

\label{fig:seq_example}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Ground-truth belief states.}
At each position $t$, the Bayesian-optimal agent maintains a joint belief
$(\pi(t), \eta_A(t), \eta_B(t), \eta_C(t))$, where $\pi(t) \in \Delta^2$ is
the meta-belief over components and $\eta_k(t) \in \Delta^2$ is the
within-component belief for component $k$.
Both are initialised to the uniform distribution and updated after each
observed token using the two-step rule (Section~\ref{sec:math}).
These analytically computed belief states are stored alongside each sequence
and used as regression targets in the geometry analysis.
50{,}000 training sequences and 5{,}000 validation sequences were generated.

Figure~\ref{fig:joint} illustrates the full joint 9-state belief geometry:
the meta-belief $\pi$ lives on the central simplex, while each vertex hosts
the MSP fractal of the corresponding component.
The three fractals are disconnected, reflecting the non-ergodic block structure.

\begin{figure}[h]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.6\textwidth]{../figures/joint_9state_attractor.png}
\caption{Joint 9-state belief geometry.
The centre shows the meta-belief $\pi$ simplex (which component?);
each vertex hosts the MSP fractal attractor of that component (where within?).
The three fractals are disconnected---no probability mass flows between

\label{fig:joint}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Architecture}

Small GPT-2-style transformer via TransformerLens~\cite{nanda2022tl}:
2~layers, $d_\mathrm{model}=64$, 4~heads, $d_\mathrm{head}=16$,
$d_\mathrm{mlp}=256$, context window 16, vocabulary $\{0,1,2\}$, $\approx$101k
parameters.

\subsection{Training}

Cross-entropy next-token prediction, AdamW ($\mathrm{lr}=10^{-3}$, cosine
decay to $10^{-5}$, 1{,}000 warmup steps), 50{,}000 steps total.
Sequences are generated on-the-fly each step to prevent overfitting.
Checkpoints saved at steps 100, 500, 1{,}000, 2{,}000, then every 5{,}000
steps; at each checkpoint we run regression at the final layer and final
position to track how $R^2$ evolves over training.

\section{Hypotheses and Predictions}
\label{sec:predictions}
% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Mathematical Derivation}
\label{sec:math}

\textbf{The joint belief state.}
The joint hidden state space is $\mathcal{S}_\mathrm{joint} = \mathcal{S}_A
\sqcup \mathcal{S}_B \sqcup \mathcal{S}_C$ (9 states).
The joint token-labeled transition matrices are block-diagonal:
\[
T^{(x)}_\mathrm{joint} = \mathrm{diag}\!\left(T_A^{(x)},\, T_B^{(x)},\,
T_C^{(x)}\right).
\]
The zeros off the block diagonal encode non-ergodicity exactly: no probability
mass can flow between components.
This block structure means the joint belief state $\eta_\mathrm{joint} \in
\Delta^8$ decomposes uniquely as
\[
\eta_\mathrm{joint}[k, s] = \pi_k \cdot \eta_k[s],
\]
where $\pi \in \Delta^2$ is the \emph{meta-belief} and $\eta_k \in \Delta^2$
is the \emph{within-component belief}.

\textbf{The two-step update.}
Upon observing token $x$:
\begin{align}
L_k(x) &= \eta_k \cdot T_k^{(x)} \cdot \mathbf{1}
          \quad \text{(per-component likelihood)}, \label{eq:likelihood}\\
\pi'_k &= \frac{\pi_k \cdot L_k(x)}{\sum_{j} \pi_j L_j(x)}
          \quad \text{(meta-belief Bayes update)}, \label{eq:pi-update}\\
\eta'_k &= \frac{\eta_k \cdot T_k^{(x)}}{L_k(x)}
           \quad \text{(within-component Bayes update)}. \label{eq:eta-update}
\end{align}
Crucially, equation~\eqref{eq:eta-update} applies the standard single-Mess3
update \emph{independently} to each component.
The components couple only through the normalisation constant
\[
Z \;=\; \sum_{j} \pi_j\, L_j(x) \;=\; P(x_t = x),
\]
which is the marginal probability of the observed token under the
mixture, in equation~\eqref{eq:pi-update}.

\textbf{Conditional independence.}
Conditioned on component identity $k$, the within-component beliefs $\eta_j$
for $j \neq k$ evolve independently of $\eta_k$.
This is the condition under which Shai et al.~\cite{shai2025factored}
(Theorem~1) proves that factored representations are lossless: no information
is lost by encoding $\pi$ and each $\eta_k$ in separate orthogonal subspaces.

\textbf{Dimensionality prediction.}
The full belief space is 8-dimensional ($\dim \Delta^8 = 8$).
Under H2 (factored), PCA should reveal 8 principal components with
non-negligible variance, structured as 4 pairs:
a 2D $\pi$ subspace (simplex, not fractal) and three 2D $\eta_k$ subspaces
(each a Mess3 fractal).

\textbf{Synchronisation horizon.}
At the start of a sequence the reader has no information about which
component is generating tokens: $\pi(0) = (1/3, 1/3, 1/3)$.
Each sequence is generated by a single component $k \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(\{A,B,C\})$,
but the component identity is hidden---only the tokens are observed.
Each observed token provides a small amount of evidence---component~A mostly
emits token~0, while component~C mixes all three freely---so $\pi(t)$
gradually sharpens toward one vertex of the simplex.
More precisely, Bayes' rule updates $\pi(t)$ by multiplying each component's
weight by its likelihood $L_k(x)$, so components whose token statistics better
match the observed tokens gain weight while the others decay.
The \emph{synchronisation horizon} $N^*$ is the characteristic number of
tokens required for $\pi(t)$ to collapse near the vertex of the true
component---i.e.\ for the reader to have effectively identified which component
generated this sequence.

Crucially, collapse only happens because the components have \emph{different}
token statistics.
If all components were identical, every token would carry equal likelihood
under every component ($L_k(x) = L_{k'}(x)$ for all $k, k'$), the ratio
$\pi_k(t)/\pi_{k'}(t)$ would never change, and $\pi$ would remain uniform
forever ($N^* \to \infty$).
$N^*$ is therefore a measure of how distinguishable the components are:
the more similar they are per token (small $D_\mathrm{KL}$), the longer
identification takes.
The hardest-to-distinguish pair is the bottleneck, giving
\[
N^* = \frac{1}{\min_{k \neq k'} D_\mathrm{KL}(P_k \| P_{k'})},
\]
where $D_\mathrm{KL}$ is estimated empirically via per-token log-likelihood
ratios over long sequences.
With the chosen parameters, $N^* \approx 9.6 < 16$, so synchronisation
completes within every training sequence.

\subsection{Competing Hypotheses}

\begin{description}[leftmargin=2em]
\item[H1 (Joint):] The transformer represents $\eta_\mathrm{joint}$ as one
  undifferentiated 8D blob. High $R^2$ for all targets, but projecting out
  the $\pi$ subspace substantially degrades $\eta_k$ regression.
\item[H2 (Factored):] Four orthogonal subspaces: one 2D $\pi$ subspace and
  three 2D $\eta_k$ subspaces. Projecting out $\pi$ leaves $\eta_k$
  $R^2 \approx$ unchanged.
\item[H3 (Superposition):] $\pi$ and $\eta_k$ recoverable approximately but
  packed into fewer than 8 effective dimensions via near-orthogonal
  superposition.
\end{description}

\subsection{Intuitive Prediction}

My geometric intuition is that the residual stream should mirror the
ground-truth joint belief geometry: the \emph{nested fractal} shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:joint}, with $\pi$ occupying a central simplex and each vertex
of that simplex hosting the MSP fractal attractor of the corresponding
component.

I favoured H2 (Factored) by analogy to Shai et al.~\cite{shai2025factored},
where the setting is a \emph{parallel} joint process---at each timestep
independent subprocesses each emit a token and the observation is their
concatenation.
Our setup is \emph{sequential}---each sequence comes from one subprocess---but
the underlying math is the same: block-diagonal transition matrices,
conditionally independent factors, and a belief space that decomposes into
orthogonal subspaces.
If transformers learn factored representations in the parallel case, I expected
them to do so here too.

My one pre-registered quantitative prediction was that
\textbf{CEV dimensionality $\approx 8$} in the final layer, corresponding to
four 2D subspaces under H2.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}
% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\textbf{PCA and CEV dimensionality.}
To measure how many dimensions the transformer uses to represent the belief state,
we run PCA on the matrix of all residual stream vectors per layer, flattening
sequences and positions into rows to obtain an $(N \cdot L) \times d_\mathrm{model}$
matrix.
The principal components are ordered by variance explained.
The \textbf{CEV (cumulative explained variance) dimensionality} at threshold $p$ is
the smallest $d$ such that the top-$d$ components together explain $\geq p\%$ of
total variance---the minimum number of directions needed to capture $p\%$ of the
structure in the activations.

\textbf{Regression-based subspace identification}~\cite{shai2024belief}.
We fit a linear map $W \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times d_\mathrm{model}}$ from residual
stream activations $\mathbf{a} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_\mathrm{model}}$ (normalised to
zero mean and unit variance per dimension) to ground-truth belief targets
$\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^3$ (either $\pi$ or $\eta_k$) via Ridge regression
($\alpha = 10^{-4}$).
Linear decodability is measured by the coefficient of determination pooled across
all three simplex coordinates,
\[
R^2 = 1 - \frac{\sum_{i,j}(b_{ij} - \hat{b}_{ij})^2}
               {\sum_{i,j}(b_{ij} - \bar{b}_j)^2},
\]
reported on a held-out 20\% test split (fit on 80\%).
To identify the subspace encoding the belief, we compute the SVD $W = U\Sigma V^\top$:
the top-2 right singular vectors (rows of $V^\top$) are the 2 directions in
activation space along which the predictor varies most, spanning the dominant
2D belief-encoding subspace.
This basis is used in all subsequent overlap tests.

\textbf{Subspace overlap metric}~\cite{shai2025factored}.
Given two subspaces with orthonormal bases $Q_A \in \mathbb{R}^{k_A \times d}$ and
$Q_B \in \mathbb{R}^{k_B \times d}$ (orthonormal rows), the overlap is
\[
  \mathrm{overlap}(Q_A, Q_B) = \frac{\|Q_A Q_B^\top\|_F}{\sqrt{k_A \cdot k_B}}.
\]
The $(i,j)$ entry of $Q_A Q_B^\top$ is the cosine $\cos\theta_{ij}$ between the
$i$-th basis vector of $Q_A$ and the $j$-th basis vector of $Q_B$; the Frobenius
norm sums their squares, and dividing by $\sqrt{k_A k_B}$ normalises to $[0, 1]$
(0 = orthogonal subspaces, 1 = identical subspaces).

\textbf{Fractal visualisation.}
We visualise whether the residual stream encodes the full fractal geometry of
each component's belief attractor, not merely a summary statistic.
For each component $k$, we take residual stream activations from component-$k$
sequences at positions $t > N^*$ (after the meta-belief $\pi$ has collapsed,
so activations reflect pure component-$k$ belief states), apply the $\eta_k$
regression map to obtain predicted $\hat\eta_k \in \mathbb{R}^3$, and plot the
resulting point cloud in barycentric simplex coordinates.
We compare this cloud against the ground-truth MSP attractor, generated by
iterating the IFS $f_a(\eta) = \eta T^{(a)} / (\eta T^{(a)}\mathbf{1})$ for
80{,}000 steps.

\begin{figure}[h]

This suggests the transformer is \emph{more efficient} than the theoretical
minimum representation.
We offer three possible explanations, though we cannot confirm which dominates:
\begin{enumerate}[noitemsep]
  \item \textbf{Component C's near-degenerate attractor.}
        With $\alpha=0.55$, component C has only $\sim$9 reachable belief
        states, collapsing $\eta_C$ from a 2D subspace toward a near-discrete
        structure that requires far less than 2 dimensions.
  \item \textbf{Fractal attractors don't fill their 2D subspaces uniformly.}
        Each MSP attractor is a fractal with dimension strictly less than 2.
        Belief states cluster along lower-dimensional structures within each
        2D subspace, so PCA sees less than 2 effective dimensions per factor.
  \item \textbf{Superposition enabled by attractor sparsity.}
        Because the attractors are sparse, the transformer can pack multiple
        belief directions into shared dimensions without interference---achieving
        near-perfect $R^2 > 0.95$ with fewer dimensions than a uniform
        distribution would require.
\end{enumerate}
At 100\% CEV both layers require all 64 dimensions,
confirming a sharp transition between the dominant low-dimensional belief
subspace and a long tail of noise dimensions.

The ordering---within-component beliefs first, meta-belief later---suggests
that the transformer first learns to track the local token statistics of each
component individually (which requires only learning the Mess3 update within
each component's context), and only subsequently learns to \emph{distinguish}
which component is active (which requires comparing cross-component
likelihoods).
This ordering makes sense: per-component prediction reduces loss immediately,
while improving $\pi$ only helps once the model has already learned the
within-component structure to compare.

\section{Additional Analysis: Vary-One}
\label{sec:vary_one}
% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\textbf{Motivation.}
The vary-one analysis tests whether the learned $\eta_k$ subspaces are
\emph{component-specific}.
Under H2, each $\eta_k$ should live in its own 2D subspace.
Fitting the $\eta_k$ regression on only component-$k$ sequences (fixing
component identity, varying within-component positions) reveals whether the
model has learned three distinct fractal geometries or one shared one.
If the cross-component subspace overlap is low, each component occupies a
distinct region of residual stream space.

\section{Additional Analysis: Dimension Sweep}
\label{sec:dim_sweep}
% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\textbf{Motivation.}
The main experiment uses $d_\mathrm{model}=64$, giving the model roughly $8\times$
more residual stream dimensions than the theoretical minimum for a full factored
representation ($K+1$ subspaces of 2D each $= 8$ dimensions for $K=3$).
This raises the question: is the inductive bias toward factored representations
an artefact of surplus capacity, or does it persist under pressure?

Separately, we ask which belief state the model prioritises when it \emph{cannot}
represent both.
The factored representation result of Shai et al.~\cite{shai2025factored} says
$\pi$ and $\eta_k$ are equally important in the sense of conditional independence,
but they are not equally important for the \emph{next-token prediction task}.
Knowing $\pi$ alone does not determine the next-token distribution---one also needs
$\eta_k$ for the active component.
Conversely, $\eta_k$ (plus implicit knowledge of which component is active from
context) is sufficient.
After position $N^*$, the context itself encodes component identity, making $\pi$
in the residual stream redundant; $\eta_k$ must be maintained throughout.

We therefore expect that under capacity pressure the model encodes $\eta_k$ first.

\textbf{Setup.}
We train four additional models with $d_\mathrm{model} \in \{2, 4, 6, 8\}$,
keeping all other hyperparameters fixed (2 layers, $n_\text{heads} \times d_\text{head}
= d_\text{model}$ throughout, see Table~\ref{tab:dim_configs}).
Each model is trained for 20{,}000 steps on the pre-saved 50{,}000 training sequences
via a DataLoader (shuffled, cycled), which avoids the per-step HMM generation
overhead that dominates for tiny models.
Note $d=8$ is the theoretical minimum capacity for a full factored representation.

\textbf{Interpretation.}
The result confirms the prediction: $\eta_k$ is prioritised over $\pi$ under
capacity pressure.
The explanation is information-theoretic.
The residual stream should encode what the context does not already make obvious.
$\pi$ collapses after $N^* \approx 9.6$ tokens---the context sequence implicitly
carries the component identity after that point.
$\eta_k$, by contrast, requires ongoing Bayesian integration and never becomes
obvious from the raw tokens; it must be maintained explicitly in the residual
stream throughout the sequence.
Furthermore, $\eta_k$ directly determines the next-token distribution
($P(x_{t+1}) \approx \eta_k \cdot T_k^{(x_{t+1})} \cdot \mathbf{1}$ after
synchronisation), while $\pi$ is a routing variable whose gradient signal vanishes
once synchronisation is complete.
At small $d$, the loss gradient therefore drives the model to encode $\eta_k$
first, leaving $\pi$ as a low-priority secondary representation.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Which Hypothesis Best Fits?}

The picture is mixed: H2 holds cleanly for the $\pi$--$\eta$ split but not
for the internal structure of the $\eta_k$ subspaces.

\textbf{H2 confirmed for $\pi$ vs.\ $\eta_k$.}
The projection tests are decisive: removing the $\pi$ subspace from activations
causes $R^2$ drops of less than 0.001 for all $\eta_k$ targets, and removing
any $\eta_k$ subspace leaves $\pi$ regression unchanged.
The $\pi$--$\eta_k$ subspace overlaps (0.17--0.19) confirm near-orthogonality.
The transformer has cleanly separated meta-belief from within-component belief
into distinct residual stream directions.

\textbf{H2 not confirmed for $\eta_k$ vs.\ $\eta_{k'}$.}
H2 predicts four mutually orthogonal subspaces, but the three $\eta_k$
subspaces overlap substantially with each other (0.53--0.69).
This is more consistent with H3 (Superposition): the model does not dedicate a
separate 2D subspace to each component's fractal, but instead packs the three
within-component beliefs into a shared, partially overlapping region of
residual stream space.
The vary-one analysis shows that component-specific fits are more distinct
(A--C overlap 0.21) than the global fits suggest, so the components are not
fully merged---but they are far from orthogonal.

The pre-registered prediction (CEV dimensionality $\approx 8$) was not confirmed:
both layers use only 5--6 dimensions to explain 95\% of variance, consistent with
the compressed $\eta_k$ representation described above.

A notable surprise in the training dynamics: within-component beliefs ($\eta_k$)
emerge early (step~100, $R^2 > 0.9$), while the meta-belief ($\pi$) lags behind
(step~2{,}000).
This suggests the model first learns to track per-component token statistics
(which reduces loss immediately) and only subsequently learns to distinguish
\emph{which} component is active (which requires cross-component comparison).

\subsection{Implications}

\textbf{Non-ergodic pretraining induces partial factoring.}
The transformer cleanly separates ``what component am I in?'' ($\pi$) from
``where within this component am I?'' ($\eta$) without explicit supervision.
However, the three within-component beliefs are not further separated from each
other---they share residual stream directions, suggesting that factoring happens
at the coarsest level of the hierarchy (meta vs.\ within) but not below it.

\textbf{Two-level belief structure maps onto two-phase training dynamics.}
Within-component geometry is learned first and meta-belief routing emerges
second---the ordering reflects loss reduction efficiency, not conceptual
priority.

\textbf{In-context learning has geometric structure.}
ICL in real language models may involve a $\pi$-like routing circuit that
activates domain-specific $\eta$-like representations.
The factored geometry found here suggests these circuits can be linearly
separated in residual stream space.

\subsection{Limitations}

Small model ($d=64$) and short context ($L=16$) may limit generality.
The symmetric 3-state/3-token structure makes components easier to distinguish
than typical natural language domains.
Ground-truth belief computation uses exact analytical updates---real corpora do
not provide such ground truth.
